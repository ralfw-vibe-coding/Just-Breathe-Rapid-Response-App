# Dimensions of breathwork techniques

## Response

### DIRECT RESPONSE
A direct response is needed in moments of heightened or extreme overwhelm, stress or panic. Physical, tangible and immediate down regulation techniques are the most effective in these scenarios.
Examples of a direct response:

• Physiological sigh
• Humming breath
• Extended exhale

### GRADUAL RESPONSE
A gradual response is best utilised to shift the breath and regulate the nervous system over a period
of time. A gradual response is synonymous with a sequence or structured practice, or implementing specific techniques through a series of rounds or ratios. This could be either down regulating or up regulating.
Examples of a gradual response:

- ABC123 techniques and protocols

### Up

#### Energising breath
This practice invites the participant to constrict the epiglottis in order to wash the breath across the back of the throat, helping to regulate the breath, warms and energise the body and calm the mind.
HOW TO:
1. Hold your palm in front of your mouth and breath air
 out in the same way that you would if you were fogging
 up a mirror, creating a gentle washing sound. Notice
 the activation at the back of your throat
2. Keep your hand where it is and inhale making the same
 sound
3. Practice for a few rounds
4. Now, begin to use this same activation of the throat
 but seal your lips on the exhale and breath out through
 your nose
5. Practice for a few rounds
6. Now, close your mouth and breath in and out through
 the
 nose so that the sound of the breath is the same on
 the inhale and exhale
7. Practice for one minute and release


#### Cleansing and clearing
A breathing technique in which the exhalation is active and the inhalation is passive. Creating heat in the body and
strengthening the abdomen, as well as aiding digestion and clearing the nadis.
HOW TO:
1. Find a comfortable seat
2. Bring your awareness to you lower belly
3. Take a long breath in through your nose
4. Contract the muscles in the abdomen in quick but
 regulated succession to force out short exhalations;
 the inhalation will be a passive reaction to your
 active exhalation
5. Continue this active exhalation for one minute
6. Inhale deeply through the nostrils and exhale slowly
 through the mouth


#### Bellows breath
A breathing technique in which both the inhalation
and exhalation come from active contractions of the abdominals and diaphragm. Helping to strengthen the abdomen, drain excess mucus from the lungs, and energise both the body and mind.
HOW TO:
1. Find a comfortable seat
2. Take a long breath in and out through the nose
3. Take another long breath in and exhale by quickly
 contracting the abdominal muscles
4. Follow this with a quick diaphragmatic inhale so
 that the breath moves into and out of the lungs in
 quick succession
5. Continue for one minute
6. When you have finished take a long slow breath in
 through the nose and out through the mouth


#### 3-Part interrupted inhale
Brief pauses are taken during the inhale. The practice introduces the concept of expanding the length and capacity of the inhalation.
HOW TO:
1. Find a comfortable seat or reclined position
2. Take a few clearing breaths
3. On the next inhale fill the lungs to just one third of their
 capacity
4. Take a short pause
5. Fill the next third
6. Take a short pause
7. Fill the final third, or until the lungs are full with air
8. Take a final short pause
9. Exhale fully


#### Lion’s breath
Lion’s Breath is a technique to clear stale air in the body and stimulate the senses. Used in practice to immediate de-stimulate and reduce stress in the body.
HOW TO:
1. Lean the upper body forward slightly
2. Inhale through your nose with your mouth closed.
3. Open your mouth wide and stick out your tongue as
 much as you can, curling it down toward your chin.
4. Exhale forcefully with a “ha” sound, feeling the breath
 come from deep within and passing across your
 entire tongue.
5. Repeat 5-10 times


#### Circular nostril breathing (warming and energising)
Breathing practice in which we inhale only through the right nostril and exhale through the left. Encourages flow of breath through the respiratory system creating heat and energy within the body. Strengthens energy, willpower and enthusiasm.
HOW TO:
1. Find a comfortable seat
2. Lift your right hand to the nose
3. Use your ring finger to close off your left nostril and
 breath in through your right nostril
4. Close the right nostril with your thumb, releasing left
 nostril and exhale through the left nostril
5. Continue in this way, inhaling through the right nostril
 and exhaling through the left for 1-3 minutes


#### Opposite alternate nostril breathing (inhale)
A breathing technique in which the participant inhales through alternating nostrils whilst exhaling through both. Helps to calm and focus the mind, as well as an aid to balance emotions.
HOW TO:
1. Find a comfortable seat
2. Lift your right hand to the nose
3. Use your thumb to seal off your right nostril and breath
 in through your left nostril
4. Release the seal and exhale through both nostrils
5. Seal your left nostril using your ring finger and inhale
 through your right nostril
6. Release the seal and exhale again through both
7. This is one round, continue for 10 rounds


#### Elephant breath
This breathing practice is a dynamic and invigorating technique to stimulate and energise the body. This technique helps to increases oxygen levels in the bloodstream, temporarily stimulating the sympathetic nervous system, circulating more blood flow, and gently heating the physical body.
HOW TO:
1. Stand with your feet shoulder width apart, knees
 slightly bent
2. Inhale quickly through your nose as you swing your
 arms forward and up overhead
3. Exhale quickly through your nose as you bend your
 knees and fold forward, keep your arms heavy
4. Repeat for a few rounds
5. Pause in stillness and come back to your
 natural breath


#### Breath of joy
Breath of Joy is a dynamic breathing technique designed to invigorate the body and uplift the sensation response in the body. In this practice, deep inhalations are accompanied by vigorous arm movements, followed by a forceful exhalation through the mouth. This rhythmic breathing pattern floods the body with oxygen, awakening the senses and energizing the mind. It releases tension, enhances circulation, and
promotes mental clarity.
HOW TO:
1. Stand with your feet shoulder width apart, knees
 slightly bent
2. Inhale one third of the breath through your nose as you
 swing your arms forward and up overhead
3. Inhale the next third of the breath through your nose
 as you swing your arms out to the side
4. Inhale to full capacity through your nose as you swing
 your arms forward and up overhead
5. Take a big exhale through your mouth to the sound
 of ‘ha’ as you bend your knees and fold forward, arms
 move back
6. Repeat for a few rounds


#### Horse stance breathing
Stand with the legs spread and the knees bent. Place one hand resting on each knee while leaning forward allowing the abdomen to drop/hang forward. This body position allows easy access to the abdomen engagement without restriction. This position is best used cleansing and clearing breaths and other energising breathing practices.
HOW TO:
1. Stand with legs spread and knees bent
2. Place one hand resting on each knee while
 leaning forward
3. Allow the abdomen to drop/hang forward

### Down

#### Physiological sigh
The physiological sigh, or often called the cyclic sigh (when done in succession) is simple and effective down regulating, stress response breath.
HOW TO:
1. Start by noticing the current state your breath
 quality
2. Take a full inhale through the nose
3. Take second inhale through the nose, at a lower
 capacity but filling the intake to maximum capacity
4. Pause at the top
5. Open the mouth and sigh with an extended exhale
6. Repeat 5-10 times


#### Extended exhale
This breathing pattern is a release technique to help quickly down regulate the nervous system and activate the parasympathetic nervous system. It is a slow controlled technique where the exhale is longer than the inhale and no breath-holding in between. This technique is helps with relaxation and tension decompression.
HOW TO:
1. Breathe slowly to an comfortably full level
2. No pause needed
3. Exhale long, slow and deep with the mouth open
 and an audible sigh
4. Repeat as needed


#### Triangle breathing
A three part technique that helps slow down the breathing cycle and down regulate the nervous system. Triangle breathing focuses evenly on the inhale, pause at the top of the breath and the exhale. This pattern is known to help with stress and anxiousness.
HOW TO:
1. Find a comfortable seat
2. Notice the current pace of the breath,
 regardless the state
3. Take a conscious breath to begin
4. Breathe in slowly for the count of 3 seconds
 or slow counting
5. Pause at the top of the inhale for 3 seconds
 or slow counting
6. Exhale slowly for the count of 3 seconds or slow
 counting
7. Repeat this for 10-12 rounds or 2 minutes
8. If a 3 count patterns feels too short/long or not
 supportive, increase or decrease to a count that
 creates calm and ease
9. Once completed, release the technique and
 return to a natural breathing pattern


#### 3 Part breathing
A breathing technique for deep breathing that increasing oxygen intake and a full body sense of calmness. This technique helps to create both internal movement of the breath while a sense of warming within the body. Belly/chest breathing can be used effectively to calm the nerves or awaken the senses.
HOW TO:
1. Find a comfortable seat
2. Notice the current pace of the breath, regardless
 the state
3. Take a conscious breath to begin
4. Place one hand on the belly and one hand on the
 chest
5. Breathe slowly and fully into both belly and chest
 equally, feeling both hands rise and expand
6. Pause at the top of the breath for 1 second
7. Exhale slowly letting the belly and chest empty
8. Repeat this for 10 rounds or 60 seconds



#### 4 - 7- 8 Breathing
Triangle Breathing Variation
This technique is an inverted variation of the triangle breathing technique, it was developed by an internationally renowned doctor and integrative medicine pioneer,
Dr. Andrew Weil, and according to Weil, it is the most effective way of managing anxiety and quelling errant stress responses. These breathing ratio is useful for combating anxiety because they help fortify the connection between mind and body. As you slow your breathing
and focus fully on the breaths, you’re taking, and your parasympathetic nervous system becomes engaged.
HOW TO:
1. Use the triangle breathing technique above as the
 foundation
2. Inhale for the count of 4 seconds or slow counting
3. Pause and hold the breath for the count of 7 seconds
 or slow counting
4. Exhale for the count of 8 seconds or slow counting
5. Repeat this for 10-12 rounds or 2 minutes


#### Pinhole breath
The pinhole breath is a technique that is effectively used to slow down the exhalation, down regulate the nervous system and reduce real time stress.
HOW TO:
1. Find a comfortable seat
2. Notice the breath without changing it
3. Take a full inhale through the nose
4. Part your lips slightly
5. Exhale out slowly through your lips like exhaling
 through a small pinhole
6. Pause at the end of the breath
7. Repeat 10 times or 60 seconds


#### Low frequency humming breath
A gentle humming down regulation technique. The vibrations from this breathing technique are known to soothe the nervous system and to free the mind of agitation.
HOW TO:
1. Gently place the thumbs over each tragus, applying
 gentle pressure
2. Allow the rest of your fingers to rest naturally along
 your forehead
3. With mouth closed take a slow breath in to full capacity
4. As you exhale slowly create a gentle humming sound
5. Repeat as needed

This breathing practice requires the participant to be able to curl their tongue like a straw in order to draw the breath across the tongue. This cools the body, improves focus and reduces agitation, anger and anxiety.

#### Inspired pause
This breathing technique focuses on slowing the oxygen intake and exhalation. Too often when we breath we think value comes only from what we are doing, when there is significant value in moments when we are not breathing in or out. A significant and intentional pause aims to create a sense of non-physical ease, slow the heart rate down and expand the lung capacity.
HOW TO:
1. Find a comfortable seat
2. Take a conscious breath to begin
3. Breathe in slowly filling the breath to a comfortable
 and manageable level
4. Without force, pause the breath without distinct
 muscular engagement
5. Hold for the count of 2 seconds or slow counting
6. Exhale gently
7. Increase/decrease pause/hold count as preferred

### Functional

#### Conscious breath
A conscious breath is a slow considered breath to bring awareness into the body. A conscious breath is not dictated by quantity, but rather of a quality inhale and exhale to signal a focus and intention of the moment. Use this breath when beginning a practice or as a way to centre or reset into a state of complete awareness in the moment.
HOW TO:
1. Sitting or standing, bring your attention completely
 onto the breath
2. Take a conscious breath in (does not need to be deep)
3. Exhale the breath out at ease (mouth open or close)
4. Repeat as needed
5. Repeat this for 10 rounds or 60 seconds

#### Recovery breath
This technique is a variation of the conscious breath used intentionally after a breathing round. The recovery breath, or return to natural breath is to ensure that when a practice round completes, we are not simply stopping, but rather, transitioning ourselves intentionally back to a natural breath.
HOW TO:
1. At the completion of the last breath in the round,
 take a full inhale in
2. Hold the breath and pause to acknowledge the
 transition
3. Exhale fully with an extended breath helping to
 initiate a down regulation
4. The exhale can be mouth closed or open
5. Allow breathing pattern to return to a natural
 breath pace and flow


#### Diaphragmatic breathing
Diaphragmatic breathing, also known as belly breathing is a deep breathing exercise that fully engages the diaphragm and increases the efficiency of the lungs.
This is a foundational technique for helping to regulate the nervous system and create a parasympathetic response. Belly breathing can be used pro actively to create a calming effect, and as a response in times of heightened stress or anxiousness.
HOW TO:
1. Find a comfortable seat or symmetrical
 standing position
2. Notice the current pace of the breath,
 regardless the state
3. Take a conscious breath to begin
4. Place one hand on the belly, and one hand on the chest
5. Breathe slowly and fully into the belly, letting it expand
6. Pause at the top of the breath for 1 second
7. Exhale slowly letting the belly the empty
8. Repeat this for 10 rounds or 60 seconds


#### Costal breath or chest breathing
Costal breathing is a mode of breathing that requires contraction of the intercostal muscles. As the intercostal muscles relax, air passively leaves the lungs. This type of breathing is also known as shallow breathing.
HOW TO:
1. Find a comfortable seat or symmetrical
 standing position
2. Notice the current pace of the breath,
 regardless the state
3. Take a conscious breath to begin
4. Breathe slowly into the chest feeling the
 expansion of the intercostal muscles
5. Exhale slowly letting the chest empty
6. Repeat to 10 rounds or 60 seconds


#### Clavicular breath or upper chest breathing
Clavicular breathing relies on the muscles of the chest, shoulders, upper back, and neck to elevate the upper rib cage and chest for each inhalation. This causes the upper chest and lungs to expand, resulting in inhalation.
HOW TO:
1. Start by noticing the natural state of the breath
2. Breathe into the upper chest and lower throat space
3. Feel an 360 expansion, rather than an up and down
 action
4. Repeat to 10 rounds or 60 seconds

#### Even breath
The steady inhalation and exhalation of equal duration.
A straightforward practice that helps to soothe both body and mind. A technique to help slow down the breathing cycle and down regulate the nervous system. Evening breathing is a comprehensive baseline for all techniques.
HOW TO:
1. Find a comfortable seat
2. Notice the current pace of the breath, regardless the
 state
3. Take a conscious breath to begin
4. Breathe in slowly for the count of 4 seconds or
 slow counting
5. Take a natural pause at the top of the inhale for
 approximately 1 second
6. Exhale to the count of 4 seconds or slow counting
7. Repeat this for 30 rounds or 2 minutes
8. If a 4 count patterns feels too short, long or not
 supportive increase or decrease to a count that
 creates calm and ease
9. Once completed, release the technique and return to
 a natural breathing pattern

#### 360 Breathing
This circumferential technique is a focused variation of belly/chest breathing that emphasises full torso breath capacity. Use 360 degree breathing to encourage a full breath and oxygen distribution and even inflation of rib cage expansion.
HOW TO:
1. Find a comfortable seat
2. Place one hand on the belly and one hand on the chest
3. Breathe slowly and fully into both belly and chest
 equally, feeling both hands rise and expand
4. Focus on expanding evening in a 360 degree direction
5. Pause at the top of the breath for 1 second
6. Exhale slowly letting the belly and chest empty
7. Repeat this for 10 rounds or 60 seconds

#### Coherent breath
Coherent breathing is to breath at a rate of 5.5 breaths per minute, which generally translates into inhaling and exhaling to the count of 5.5 seconds. The purpose of this type of breathing is to increase heart rate variability.
HOW TO:
1. Inhale into your belly for five seconds
2. Exhale for another five seconds
3. Repeat for three to five minutes. Please mind the
 following: Avoid breathing like a robot, or like you’re
 scuba diving - breathe naturally as you usually do, just
 slower

#### 365 Method
365 Method is a common technique often recommended by clinical professionals to counter accumulated stress. This method is a recommendation to breathe and practice repeat all 365 days of the year.
HOW TO:
1. Using coherent breathing
2. Breathing practices three times a day
3. Breathing six times per minute
 (inhale for 5, exhale for 5)
4. Repeat for five minutes

Establish a solid foundation for your breath with this foundational practice. Incorporating even breath, triangle breath, and box breath (remember this using the acronym ‘circle, triangle, square’ in reflection of the breathing patterns), this sequence promotes respiratory efficiency and overall balance. By syncing your inhalation and exhalation, you can cultivate a sense of evenness while optimizing oxygen intake.


- Even breath
- Triangle breathing
- Box breathing

Balanced breathing exercise Return to homeostatis: a sustained balance breathing protocol
This protocol is to help return the breath to balance or maintain a balanced homeostasis. These techniques help to regulate the autonomic nervous system, enhance oxygenation, and promote mental clarity. Balanced breathing reduces stress, improves focus, and cultivates overall equilibrium.


- 360 breathing
- Inspired pause
- Alternate nostril breathing

Stress response breathing exercise Dissolve stress: a breath-based protocol
Respond to stress, anxiety, overwhelm or over stimulation with this rapid stress relief protocol and targeted breathing sequence. Combining the physiological sigh, extended exhale, and coherent breathing, this breathing protocol effectively activates the body's relaxation response by down regulating the sympathetic nervous system.

- Physiological sigh
- Extended exhale
- Coherent breathing

### XLT
Calming breathing exercise Sustained rest: a breath-based restoration protocol
Experience a scientifically-validated approach to stress management through this breathing practice. Combining extended exhale, low frequency humming breath and 4-7-8 breathing techniques, this protocol targets the body's stress response. By influencing the autonomic nervous system and promoting oxygenation, these exercises can induce a state a calming response and a parasympathetic activation.

- Extended exhale
- Low frequency humming breath

Energising breath exercise Ignite your energy: a breath- based boost protocol

# Variations

1. COUNTING VARIATIONS

When practicing a technique we can vary the number of breaths we take. For example; in box breathing, we can change and increase from 4:4:4:4 to 6:6:6:6 or 8:8:8:8 or in cleansing and clearing breath we could increase from 1 x 30 / 1 x 60 / 1 x 90.

2. TOUCH VARIATIONS

When practicing with touch variation we can use our hands to create different breath focus. For example in 3-part breath we can practice, both hands on belly, one hand of belly and one of chest and then finally, hands rest in the lap (no touch.)

3. NUMBER OF ROUNDS VARIATIONS

When practicing with rounds variation we can vary the quantity of repetitive amount of cycles per technique execution. For example in cleansing & clearing breath we could change and increase each round from 1 x 30 / 3 x 30 / 5 x 30.

4. THE GAZE VARIATIONS

When practicing with the variations of the gaze, we are speaking about where the eyes are or how we place our vision. For example in the even breath, we can give variation by practicing; eyes open, narrowed gaze, eyes closed.

5. SPEED & PACE VARIATIONS

When practicing with speed & pace we can change the timing and space between the breaths. For example in cleansing & clearing breathe we could change the pace from 1 x 30 slow / 1 x 30 moderate / 1 x 30 fast.

6. ENERGY & EFFORT VARIATIONS

When practicing with energy and effort variations we can adapt the heat and enthusiasm within the chosen technique. For example with the extended exhale breath your could offer options of gentle exhale, intentional clearing, forced clearing.

7. NOSE & MOUTH VARIATIONS

When practicing with nose & mouth variation we have the option of directing or redirecting the air flow. For example, in the interrupted inhale we can vary the extended exhale from mouth closed, to mouth open to a sign.

8. SUPPORT VARIATIONS

When practicing with support variations we are offering supportive or held variations. For example in alternate nostril breathing you practice; standard offering, non-used hand tucked across the body in the arm pit, and finally both hands tucked across the body (with no hands on nose and practicing subtly.)

9. VOLUME VARIATIONS

When practicing with volume variation we can alter the sound or the breath in audible techniques. For example in the humming breath, we could vary at volumes of quiet, moderate to loud.

10. SEAT POSITIONS VARIATIONS

When practicing with a variation in your seat, is to simply change your seat position. In many practices you could offer from; seated, reclined or rested on the floor as a practice progression.
